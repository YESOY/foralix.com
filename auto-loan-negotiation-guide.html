Step 1: Get Pre-Approved (Your Secret Weapon)

üí° Why Pre-Approval Matters

Getting pre-approved from banks or credit unions before visiting dealerships gives you negotiating power and a baseline rate to compare against dealer financing.

Where to Get Pre-Approved:

  • Credit Unions: Typically offer the lowest rates
  • Online Lenders: Competitive rates, easy comparison
  • Local Banks: Relationship-based rates
  • Dealer Financing: Use as leverage, not primary option

Step 3: Master the Dealership Negotiation

‚úÖ DO These at the Dealership:

  • Keep negotiations separate: Price, trade-in, and financing should be discussed separately
  • Use your pre-approval as leverage: "I have 4.5% financing already - can you beat this?"
  • Focus on the loan term: Shorter terms usually have lower rates
  • Ask about manufacturer incentives: Often include special financing rates

‚ùå DON'T Do These at the Dealership:

  • Don't discuss monthly payment first: Focus on the total loan amount and interest rate
  • Don't fall for "what monthly payment can you afford?": This hides the true cost

  • Don't feel pressured to use dealer financing: You can always use your pre-approval

Step 4: Negotiate These Key Loan Terms

1. Interest Rate (APR)

Strategy: Start 1-2% below your pre-approval rate and negotiate upward if needed.

2. Loan Term

Strategy: Opt for the shortest term you can afford. A 48-month loan typically has better rates than 72-month.

3. Down Payment

Strategy: Larger down payments often secure better rates and reduce total interest.

4. Fees and Add-ons

Strategy: Question every fee. Many are negotiable or can be removed.

Common Dealer Tactics and How to Counter Them

üö® "The Four Square Method"

What it is: Dealers use a four-square worksheet to confuse buyers by mixing trade-in value, down payment, monthly payment, and vehicle price.

Your counter: Insist on negotiating each item separately. Focus on the out-the-door price first.

üö® "The Payment Shoppers"

What it is: Dealers focus only on monthly payment to hide high interest rates and long terms.

Your counter: Ask for the interest rate, loan term, and total financed amount separately.

üö® "The Bump"

What it is: After you agree on terms, the finance manager says they need manager approval and comes back with worse terms.

Your counter: Be prepared to walk away. Have your pre-approval ready as backup.

Special Situations: New vs Used Cars

New Car Financing

  • Manufacturer incentives: Often include 0-2.9% financing for qualified buyers
  • Longer terms available: Up to 84 months (but avoid if possible)
  • Better rates generally: Lower risk for lenders

Used Car Financing

  • Higher rates: Typically 1-3% higher than new cars
  • Shorter terms: Usually max 60-72 months
  • Age/mileage limits: Rates increase for older/high-mileage vehicles
